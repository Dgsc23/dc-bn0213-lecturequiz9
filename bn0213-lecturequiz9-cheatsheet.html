The cholesterol molecule

"You should know that there's four rings, the A, B, C, and D rings."
Steroid nucleus
The core is four fused rings, labeled A, B, C, and D. Basso said you do not need to draw it, but you should know it is these four rings.
Two methyl groups
Besides the four rings, the bare steroid skeleton carries just two methyl groups (the angular methyls). He called the skeleton pretty simple.
What makes it cholesterol: OH
Cholesterol adds a hydroxyl (OH) group on ring A. He would not ask which carbon, only that cholesterol carries this hydroxyl.
What makes it cholesterol: tail
It also adds an 8-carbon branched hydrocarbon side chain off the D ring (at C17), plus a double bond on ring B.
Free vs esterified
About 30% is free cholesterol (poorly water-soluble, sits in plasma membranes) and about 70% is esterified. The ester form is even less water-soluble, so it is the form used to transport cholesterol in blood (serum cholesterol).

What cholesterol is for

"So it modulates fluidity."
Membrane fluidity
Cholesterol wedges between the phospholipids of the plasma membrane so they sit neither too far apart nor too close together, keeping the membrane stable yet flexible. Basso summed it up as modulating fluidity and called it really important.
Precursor molecule
Cholesterol is the precursor for bile acids, steroid hormones, and vitamin D.
Steroid hormones
The example steroid hormones that need cholesterol are testosterone and estrogen. Very low cholesterol could reduce their production.
Vitamin D
Cholesterol is the starting material for vitamin D, but ultraviolet B (UVB) sunlight is what actually manufactures it. Red light will not do it, which is why deficiency is common in northern winters.

De novo cholesterol synthesis

"So rate-limiting enzyme for cholesterol synthesis is right there, HMG-CoA reductase."
Starting block
Synthesis starts from acetyl-CoA: acetyl-CoA + acetoacetyl-CoA form HMG-CoA. Basso repeats that acetyl-CoA is the way to many things.
Needed but NOT rate-limiting
HMG-CoA synthase is required to build HMG-CoA, but it is not the rate-limiting enzyme. Do not confuse it with the reductase.
Rate-limiting enzyme
HMG-CoA reductase is the rate-limiting (regulatory) enzyme of cholesterol synthesis. This is the one to know.
Product
HMG-CoA reductase converts HMG-CoA to mevalonate. Know the name mevalonate; he said you do not need its structure or the isoprenoid steps after it.

Regulating cholesterol synthesis

"then the cholesterol will negatively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase and you'll shut down de novo cholesterol synthesis."
Negative feedback
When cholesterol is plentiful it negatively inhibits HMG-CoA reductase, shutting down de novo synthesis so the body does not make too much (homeostasis).
Statins
Statin drugs (e.g., Lipitor) inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the same rate-limiting enzyme cholesterol itself feedback-inhibits.
Statins and LDL receptors
Lowering the free cholesterol inside a liver cell makes it express more LDL receptors. More receptors pull LDL out of the blood, so LDL clearance rises and plasma LDL falls. Too much intracellular cholesterol does the opposite (shuts LDL-receptor genes off).

The cholesterol balance sheet

"and in fact we make two times as much de novo."
Raises cholesterol
Dietary intake (Western diet is about 0.5 g/day) plus de novo synthesis. We actually make about twice as much as we eat (~1 g/day), which is why vegans still have cholesterol.
Lowers cholesterol
Conversion to bile salts and excretion of free cholesterol in bile.
Why we like HDL
HDL returns cholesterol from the periphery back to the liver, where it is turned into bile salts. That is the cleanup side of the balance.
Fiber (and cholestyramine)
Fiber binds bile salts in the gut so they leave in the stool. Losing bile salts forces the liver to convert more cholesterol into new bile acids, which lowers cholesterol. The drug cholestyramine works the same way.

Bile acid synthesis

"So do I like rate-limiting enzymes? Yes. 7-alpha-hydroxylase."
Precursor
Bile acids are made in the liver from cholesterol. Making bile is one of the body's main ways to get rid of cholesterol.
Rate-limiting enzyme
7-alpha-hydroxylase (CYP7A1) catalyzes the first, rate-limiting step, adding an OH to carbon 7 of cholesterol (forming 7-hydroxycholesterol). Do not confuse it with HMG-CoA reductase (cholesterol synthesis) or desmolase (steroid hormones).
Two primary bile acids
Cholic acid and chenodeoxycholic acid are the two primary (unconjugated) bile acids made directly from cholesterol.
Feedback
Bile acids inhibit 7-alpha-hydroxylase (negative feedback) so the body does not overproduce them.

Bile salts: conjugation, structure and job

"released in the intestine during a meal act as detergents to emulsify dietary lipids."
Conjugation
A bile acid becomes a bile salt when the liver attaches an amino acid, glycine or taurine (e.g., cholic acid + glycine = glycocholic acid).
Why conjugate
Conjugated bile salts are more ionized (more charged), which makes them better detergents.
Amphipathic
A bile salt has a hydrophobic (cholesterol-derived) side that faces fat and a hydrophilic (salt) side that faces water, so it can surround fat droplets.
Main job
Bile salts are detergents that emulsify dietary lipids, chopping big fat globules into tiny droplets (micelles) so fat can be absorbed. Emulsify means break into smaller pieces.
Made and stored
Produced by the liver, secreted into bile, and stored in the gallbladder until a meal. You can survive without a gallbladder because the liver still makes bile.
Release signal
After a meal, cholecystokinin (CCK) makes the gallbladder contract and squeeze bile into the duodenum.

Enterohepatic circulation and elimination

"the majority is reabsorbed at the ileum and returns back to the liver again."
The ring cannot be broken
Humans cannot break down the steroid ring, so the only way to eliminate cholesterol is to convert it to bile acids and salts and to secrete free cholesterol into bile (which then leaves in the feces).
Enterohepatic circulation
After emulsifying fat, the majority of bile salts are reabsorbed in the ileum and returned to the liver via the portal vein. Less than 5% are lost in the feces, so they are recycled.
Secondary bile salts
Intestinal bacteria deconjugate (remove glycine/taurine) and dehydroxylate (remove the C7 hydroxyl) the primary bile salts, making the secondary bile salts deoxycholic and lithocholic acid. These are less soluble and less reabsorbed, so more are excreted.

Lipoproteins: the big picture

"Collectively, all of these lipoproteins are basically lipid ubers in your body."
Their job
Lipoproteins are the body's lipid Ubers. Because TAG and cholesterol are hydrophobic, they cannot travel free in watery blood, so lipoproteins package and transport them.
Structure
A hydrophobic core of triacylglycerols and cholesterol esters, wrapped in a surface of phospholipids, free cholesterol, and apoproteins (polar heads facing the blood).
Density order
Chylomicron (lowest density, most fat) → VLDL → IDL → LDL → HDL (highest density, most protein). Fat is less dense than protein, so more fat = lower density.

The apolipoproteins (memorize these)

"Apo-E is the key to the liver."
ApoB-48
Unique to chylomicrons (from the intestinal enterocyte). Mnemonic: B-52 bomber, big plane, big particle.
ApoB-100
On VLDL, IDL, and LDL (the liver-derived line). It is the tag the LDL receptor recognizes.
ApoC-II
Activates lipoprotein lipase (LPL) so the particle can unload its triglycerides. Mnemonic: C-II looks like two Ls for Lipoprotein Lipase.
ApoE
The key to the liver: lets chylomicron remnants and IDL be recognized and taken up by liver cells.
ApoA-1
On HDL: recovers cholesterol from fatty streaks in vessels and activates LCAT to form cholesterol esters.

Chylomicrons and VLDL

"The difference is the VLDL gets made in that liver."
Chylomicron: origin and cargo
Assembled in the intestinal enterocytes from the dietary fat you just ate (a triacylglycerol-rich big ball of dietary lipids). Its tag is apoB-48.
Lipoprotein lipase (LPL)
LPL sits on the endothelium of peripheral tissues, not the liver, and releases fatty acids from the particle's triglycerides (muscle burns them, adipose stores them). LPL is activated by apoC-II.
VLDL
Made in the liver, carries apoB-100, and ferries mostly triglycerides out to the tissues, working like a chylomicron.

IDL, LDL and delivering cholesterol

"So if you're ever asked, which of the lipoproteins has the highest concentration of cholesterol?"
VLDL to IDL to LDL
As VLDL drops off triglycerides it becomes IDL (the VLDL remnant), which is then converted to LDL.
LDL: highest cholesterol
Of all the lipoproteins, LDL has the highest cholesterol concentration (the earlier particles already shed their triglycerides). Basso flagged this as a likely exam question.
LDL: its job
LDL delivers cholesterol to peripheral tissues, using apoB-100. This delivery role plus its high cholesterol content is why excess LDL is linked to disease.
Where LDL gets cholesterol
HDL donates cholesterol esters to IDL and LDL via cholesterol ester transfer protein (CETP); the name tells you the job.

HDL: the good cholesterol

"It's returning cholesterol back to the liver."
Reverse cholesterol transport
HDL is the good cholesterol because it mops up excess cholesterol from the periphery and returns it to the liver, where it becomes bile salts. Higher HDL is good.
ApoA-1 and LCAT
ApoA-1 recovers cholesterol from fatty streaks in vessels and activates LCAT, which makes the cholesterol esters HDL carries back.
SR-B1 delivery
HDL also delivers cholesterol esters to steroidogenic tissues (adrenals, testes, ovaries) via the scavenger receptor B1 (SR-B1), supplying cholesterol for steroid hormones.

Atherosclerosis and your LDL/HDL profile

"And if you have high levels of LDL and low levels of HDL, that's not a good situation to be in."
How LDL causes plaque
LDL is not inherently bad, but in excess or when oxidized it is the problem. Oxidized LDL triggers an inflammatory response; cells take it up and become foam cells that die, and the debris builds up as atherosclerotic plaque.
The bad profile
High LDL with low HDL is the unfavorable one: lots of cholesterol delivered to tissues and too little HDL to clear it. You would rather have high HDL with low LDL.
